StrumSchool

    Two weeks ago I went to my first startup event in San Francisco, meeting music entrepreneurs who had created everything from new types of guitars to Spotify apps. StrumSchool was the most developed idea I came across, mostly because I could use a free way to learn the guitar. I have a few friends taking guitar classes that cost a fair amount of money but in their defense, it works. The guys at StrumSchool are trying to create a platform that can be a tue competitor to in-person guitar classes.

    The site currently has several tutorial videos to get you started. But the goal, as relayed to me from the founders, is to create a community in which guitar teachers can video chat live with people who want lessons, for a tiny fee compared to in person lessons. These virtual lessons will give the professionals a more efficent way of utilizing their talents while allowing students to get easier, more personal access to teachers. Any website that creates a community which is a win-win situation for both parties has my support. Definitely keep your eyes out for this one. If you start learning now, you could be teaching the classes in a few months.

[SIC] APPAREL

    A lot of things about this startup idea get me excited, not the least of which being the cute girl working for [SIC] APPAREL. These guys are looking to provide artists with a chance to get their work onto T-shirts while using that art as a way to help out others. Like most good ideas, I find this so intruiging because of its self sustainability. Websites like threadless.com have shown that people are more then willing to buy T-shirts covered in the work of independent artists, for both uniqueness and flat out style. With a charitable factor being added to the mix, this seems like a sure-fire winner.

Samasource

    I saw this video while on the TED talks website a few months ago and Samasource quickly became one of my favorite companies. Leila Janah's vision for tackling the problems of developing countries has proven to be not only novel but also effective. "Microwork", as she calls it, allows American companies the opportunity to outsource work to independent, contractor-type workers in the developing world. I couldn't agree with her more that the brain power of billions of people are being underutilized and that these minds are arguably the greatest untapped resource on Earth.

    I initially became interested in this type of idea several years ago while studying at MIT. An innovation class of mine centered around guest speakers who had experience in an innovative environment, often focused on the developing world. One lecture and question/answer session was with Iqbal Quadir, head of the Legatum Center for Development and Entrepreurship at MIT. Prior to his role founding the Legatum Center at the 'tute, Iqbal worked to create the Grameenphone, an afforable mobile phone company in Bangladesh. The focus of Quadir's idea, built of the Grameen Bank's microcredit model, was that the phone could act not just as a convenience but a source of income. The company is currently the largest mobile phone provider in Bangladesh with over 32 million subscribers and has helped the economy develop greatly over the past fifteen years. Check out Quadir's current work at the Legatum Center here.
